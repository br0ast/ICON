ICON Ontology: Description back to ToC

This section describes the fundamental characteristics of the ontology and furnishes practical examples of usage. To better describe its usage, we include a practical description of an example, that will be formally described at each level of interpretation in the corresponding sections. It is subdivided in five parts:
  1. Recognition
  2. Example description
  3. Level 1
  4. Level 2
  5. Level 3

Recognition

This section provides a description of how recognition is formally expressed, as shown in the figure. We consider individuals of the class Recognition every claim that is made by an agent (a person or a computer) about an artwork. By aligning the class Recognition to other ontologies, it is possible to carefully express its souces and evidence (CiTO), type (HiCO), and agents (DOLCE, CIDOC-CRM). The subclasses of a recognition express each interpretation conducted at a certain level of interpretation, namely Pre-Iconographical Recognition and Formal Motif Recognition (level 1), Iconographical Recognition (level 2), and Iconological Recognition (level 3).
Each recognition assigns to the object of the interpretation (the artwork or parts of it) the result of the interpretation. For example, a second level recognition assigns to the level 1 subject "child with bow and arrows" (object of interpretation) the second level subject recognized. i.e. "Cupid" (the result of the interpretation). As described in the table below, in each level of interpretation the ontology makes a distinction between the description of the subject in the specific context of the artwork and the general subject as can be described by controlled vocabularies. Therefore, the object assigned by the recognition is not the general subject but the subjects as represented in the specific artwork considered (i.e. artistic motifs, images, intrinsic meanings). In this way, it is possible to describe peculiar details that the subjects show in a specific work of art and consequently describe their variations. For example, if an artwork shows Venus wearing a veil and earrings, which are not her common attributes, it is possible to affirm that only this representation of Venus in that specific artwork (i.e. the "image" of Venus at level 2) has these characteristics, and it does not imply that veil and earrings are the usual way in which the goddess is represented. The image of Venus can then be linked to an entity representing this subject at a general level, or to the corresponding vocabulary term, as the Iconclass code 92C4.

Example description

As a way of example of ontology usage, the description of two reliefs on the external wall of Saint Mark's Basilica in Venice will be described, and their formal description will be visually represented at each level of interpretation.

This example is considered by Panofsky and Saxl[5]. In fact, they noticed that two reliefs (figures 1 and 2) belonging to different historical periods and representing different subjects have clearly the same shapes and general arrangement. The first relief is recognized by them as a late roman one, representing the story of Hercules Carrying the Herymanthian boar, whereas the second one, from the XIII Century A.D., represents an allegory of Christ saving the christian souls.

In the authors' opinion, it is evident that the medieval artist used the roman relief as a model for representing a new subject, closer to their culture. Since, during the Middle Ages, there is other evidence in which a classical artwork is used as a model for contemporary subjects, deprived of its classical content, they claim that these artworks are the evidence of a cultural phenomenon happening. They conclude that Medieval Western art was unable to retain a classical prototype without destroying its original meaning since they were not capable of realizing the unity of classical form and classical subject matter.

The table below summarizes how the artwork can be described at each level of interpretation.

In the first artwork (figure 1), a nude man with a lion skin carries on his shoulders a boar. In the lower right corner, another figure takes shelter in a barrel. At the second level of interpretation these level 1 subjects can be grouped and recognized: the man is Hercules with his attribute "lion skin" and the man in the barrel is king Eurystheus.

The same interpretative process can be done with the other artwork (figure 2), where the man is recognized as Christ, the deer as the symbol of the Christians' souls, and the dragoon in the corner is recognized as symbol of the Devil. His actions of carrying the deer and of stepping on the Devil are respectively symbolically interpreted as the act saving the souls and defeating the Devil. In the last level, the cultural phenomenon described above is recognized.

Level 1

Pre-iconographical Recognition

In a pre-iconographical recognition, the agent (interpreter) recognizes some artistic motifs in a work of art. These artistic motifs are associated phisically to a portion of the painting (called Pure form) and conceptually to Natural Elements, Actions, Qualities and Expressional Qualities.

According to Panofsky's theory [1], all the elements cited above can be part of this level of interpretation (pre-iconographical). As the term says, these are all the elements that are recognized before an iconographic interpretation. As to say, before recognizing the Virgin Mary (or at the same time), an interpreter might recognize a woman on a work of art. At the same time, before recognizing Excalibur one might recognize a sword. Natural elements in fact represent general elements that are usually recognized through our experience of the world and do not require specific iconographic knowledge. A person that might not recognize the Virgin Mary is still more likely to recognize a woman in a painting, given that they had experience in seeing how women look like in their experience of the world. A specific quality might also be recognized on this level, like for example a color or a shape of a specific element. Actions are also recognizable, such as running, fighting, and they also are recognized through our experience of the world. Finally, expressional qualities such as emotions can also be recognized on this level. Artistic motifs that represent these recognized elements can be grouped together inside compositions. A composition contains a series of artistic motif and it can also be specified how the artistic motif are grouped inside the composition through a compositional structure.

With our ontology, Pre-iconographical recognitions can be described as it follows. From the n-ary relationship class PreiconographicalRecognition, you can link the recognized artistic motif, expressed with the ArtisticMotif class, with the property recognizedArtisticMotif. A composition can also be linked to a Pre-iconographical Recognition with the property recognizedComposition. To indicate the physical portion of the painting in which said composition or artistic motif is, you can associate the Pure form (class PureForm) to the recognition through the property associatedForm. You can furtherly expand the PureForm class by adding either boundary boxes (which can be the result of an automatic recognition through computer vision algorithms) or by adding a IIIF [2] specification of the image of the painting (that selects the pure form) through the data property hasRegionDescription. A composition is linked to the Artistic Motifs that are part of it through the hasPart property. Its compositional structure (CompositionalStructure class) can be linked to it with the property hasCompositionalStructure. Finally, Artistic Motifs can be associated to the classes NaturalElement, Action, dul:Quality, ExpressionalMeaning respectively with the properties hasNaturalMeaning (for natural elements and actions), dul:hasQuality, hasExpressionalMeaning.

Example

To better explain the ontology usage, we describe how the preiconographical recognition can be applied to the first level of interpretation of the example described in the section example description.

The relief representing "Hercules carrying the Erymanthian Boar" shows, at the first level of interpretation, a man wearing a lion skin carrying on his shoulders a boar. We know that a man with a lion skin will be interpreted as the character Hercules at the second level of interpretation. Then, the man taking shelter in a barrel will be interpreted as King Euryshteus the the second level. The first man is also doing the action he makes of carrying a boar on his shoulders. Finally, the overall scene will be interpreted ad the story of Hercules carrying the Erymanthian boar to king Eurystheus.

These level 1 objects can be grouped in different compositions. A composition is hence a unity of artistic motifs that have a sense together, needed for creating a correspondence between the second and the first level subjects. Compositions can be also used for indicating the objects involved in an action as well, and it is not required that every composition is futher interpreted in the second level of interpretation. As a result, we obtain four compositions:

  • composition 1: man and lion skin
  • composition 2: man, action of carrying, boar
  • composition 3: man, taking shelter, barrel
  • composition 4: all the Artistic Motifs identified

The recognition is modelled as follows. The authors (crm:P14_carried_out_by) recognize (PreiconographicalRecognition) a Composition, which is composed (hasPart) by ArtisticMotifs, i.e. the specific manifestation in the artwork of the subjects represented. The Artistic Motifs are then related to the general subjects and qualities that are recognized. For example, to the Artistic Motif related to the subject "man" can be linked the quality (dul:Quality) "nudity". In this way, it is possible to affirm that only the man as represented in this artwork has "nudity" as quality, and not all the representations of "man" in other artworks are likely to have the same quality.

If the coordinates of the physical region in which the subjects recognized by the interpretation are known, they can be associated by the relation associatedForm to the PreiconographicalRecognition.

The text from which the intepretation is extracted can be indicated as source of the interpretation (cito:citesForInformation).

The same approach can be used to describe the second relief of the example.

Formal Motif Recognition

In a formal motif recognition, the interpreter recognizes that a specific artistic motif (or composition) might have been copied from a prototypical artistic motif of another artwork. To express this concept in ICON, from the class Formal Motif Recognition, the prototypical motif used as an inspiration is linked to it through the property hasPrototypicalMotif. The copied motif, that takes inspiration from the prototypical one, is linked to the FormalMotifRecognition class using the property hasCopiedMotif.

Multiple Formal Motif Recognitions and Pre-iconographical recognitions can be grouped together inside a Pre-iconographical Description. A pre-iconographical description thus covers all the pre-iconographical aspects of an artwork recognized by one or more interpretation(s). The class PreiconographicalDescription is linked to the classes PreiconographicalRecognition and FormalMotifRecognition through the cidoc crm property crm:P70_Documents

Both Pre-iconographical Recognitions and Formal Motif Recognitions classes and properties can be seen in the scheme representing level 1.

Example

We describe how the Formal Motif Recognition can be applied to the first level of interpretation of the example described in the section example description.

Since Saxl and Panofsky claims that the artists making the medieval relief on the Saint Mark's Basilica wall copied the shape and general arrangement from the roman one, a Formal Motif Recognition occurs. Therefore, the composition identifying all the scene represented can be related to the composition representing the classical scene. In addition, more recognitions can identify the exact correspondence between:

  • the deer and the boar, respectively identified by the artistic motifs ART2-AM3 and ART1-AM3
  • Christ and Hercules, respectively identified by the compositions ART2-COMP1 and ART1-COMP1
  • the dragoon and king Eurystheus, respectively identified by the artistic motif ART2-AM6 and ART1-COMP3

As shown in the scheme, each FormalMotifRecognition is about (aboutWorkOfArt) both the artworks involved. In this case, Saxl and Panofsky are always the authors of the recognition. The parts of the artwork taken as a prototype (e.g. the Roman relief, here indicated as ART1), namely Artistic Motifs and Composiitons, are related to the interpetation with the property hasPrototypicalMotif, whereas the corresponding parts of the final copying artwork (e.g. the Medieval relief, , here indicated as ART2) are related through the property hasCopiedMotif. In this way, the direct correspondence between level 1 levels can be expressed. All the recognitions are listed in the PreiconographicalDescriptions of both the artworks.

Level 2

Iconographical Recognition

In the second level of interpretation, an interpreter associates canonical Characters, Symbols, Events, Personifications, Named Objects to an artwork through the recognition of its Images. An Image is in fact the specific visual representation of one those aforementioned element in a painting. I.e. meanwhile there is (arguably) one Virgin Mary concept as a character, its visual representation (image) changes from painting to painting. The recognition of these elements in an image is the heart of the Iconographic Recognition. Moreover, characters, named objects (uniquely identifiable objects that are often used as an attribute for other iconographic subjects, such as Excalibur, or thor's hammer), symbols etc... take part into Invenzioni. An invenzione is defined as a coherent set of characters, events, named objects, places, symbols, personifications under a narrative purpose (in this case an invenzione is called Story) or under an allegorical purpose (in this case it is called Allegory). Stories and Allegories can also be recongnized in a painting during the iconographic recongition. Furthermore, the artistic motif that refer to the recognized images can be linked to them in order not to lose important connection between levels of interpretation. As to say it is important to preserve the fact that the image that represent the character zeus is associated to the artistic motif associated to a man in a painting, and a bull in another painting (as olympic gods are sometimes depicted as wild animals). Finally, a series of Iconographical Recognitions can be grouped together in an Iconographical Analysis in the same way that pre-iconographical recognitions were grouped in Preiconographical Descriptions.

To describe these concepts using ICON ontology we start with the N-ary relationship class IconographicalRecognition. The recognized images (Image class) are linked to it through the property recognizedImage. The recognized invenzioni (class Invenzione, with sub-classes Story and Allegory) are linked to it with the property recognizedInvenzione. Every recognition is always dedicated to one element, so one image or invenzione is permitted per recognition. An image is linked to the corresponding artistic motif (or composition) in the pre-iconographic level through the property refersToArtisticMotif. If this artistic motif (or compositon) was the pivotal element to make a certain recognition of a second level subject, then that artistic motif is linked to the image through the property hasRecAttribute. Images are linked to the subjects that they visually represent with different properties according to the type of subject:

  • Place: hasPlace
  • Character: hasCharacter
  • Personification: hasPersonification
  • NamedObject: hasNamedObject
  • Symbol: hasSymbol. Symbols can be represented as Simulations [3] in this ontology. A simulation is considered as the relationship that happens between a symbol, its symbolic meaning and the context in which the symbol denotes the symbolic meaning. For example in a painting one might recognize that there is a dog as a symbol of fidelity. The simulation dog-fidelity would be linked to the painting. Simulations are expressed through the Simulation Ontology [4]. For more information about the use of the Simulation Ontology refer to its documentation

Characters, places etc... are linked to the Invenzione in which they take part into using the property composedOf

Finally, the class IconographicalAnalysis is linked to the iconographical recognitions made on an artwork with the cidoc-crm property crm:P70_Documents

Level 3

Iconological Recognition

In the third level of interpretation, an interpreter recognizes an intrinsic meaning in a work of art. This meaning can be linked to a specific element of the artwork (Artistic Motif, Image, Composition) or the whole artefact. Moreover, this intrinsic meaning might depend on a cultural phenomenon that is reflected in the artwork itself. The recognition of these elements are what makes an iconological recognition. Multiple iconological recognitions can be grouped together in an iconographical synthesis.

The expression of these concepts in the ontology starts with the class IconologicalRecognition, that is linked to the recognized instrinsic meaning (class IntrinsicMeaning) through the property recognizedIntrinsicMeaning. The meaning itself is expressed with the cidoc class crm:E28_Conceptual_Object and it is linked to the intrinsic meaning with the property recognizedConceptualObject. The cultural phenomenon associated to the intrinsic meaning is linked to it with the property recognizedCulturalPhenomenon. At the moment, the class CulturalPhenomenon has as subclasses Tendency, Belief, Attitude, CulturalValue. No further specification for these classes is provided as they might be thoroughly described in specific ontologies to be developed. If the intrinsic meaning is recognized particularly thanks to a specific artistic motif, composition or image it is linked to them respectively with the properties hasArtisticMotif, hasComposition and hasImage. Finally, the class IconographicalSynthesis is linked to all the iconologic recognitions that compose it through the cidoc property crm:P70_Documents

Example

In the example described, it is recognized by Saxl and Panofsky that both the artworks are involved in a common cultural phenomenon. In detail, the phenomenon regards specifically the Medieval relief, since it concerns the way in which Medieval artists deal with the Classic art. Therefore, we can say that the phenomenon is embedded in the Medieval relief, and the roman one is evidence of it.

In addition, this recognition is supported by the fact that there is a copy of formal motifs done by the Medieval artist, who took the Roman relief as a prototype for his artwork. Therefore, it is possible to affirm that the Formal Motif Recognitions made at level 2 support the level 3 interpretation, identifying the cultural phenomenon.

At the third level of interpretation can be recognized also that the Allegory of Salvation expresses the concept of Redemption, as part of the meanings belonging to the Christian beliefs. Consequently, another recognition linking this deeper meaning to the artwork should be considered.

This interpretative situation can be modelled as follows (see the scheme for its representation). The authors make a third-level recognition, i.e. an IconologicalRecognition, and recognize that the Artwork representing an Allegory of Salvation has an Intrinsic Meaning (IconologicalRecognition, recognizedIntrinsicMeaning, IntrinsicMeaning). This Intrinsic Meaning is then linked to the CulturalPhenomenon of itself. The Intrinsic Meaning is indeed the specific manifestation of the general phenomenon recognized. The Motifs Recognitions already conducted at level 1 that are described in the scheme of a Formal Motif Recognition (level 1), support this interpretation by means of the relation cito:givesSupportTo. The IconologicalRecognition, then, cites as evidence (cito:citesAsEvidence) the Roman relief.

Following, another iconological recognition links to the artwork another intrinsic meaning, which is related to the concept of "redemption". Since this interpretation can be seen in the second-level subjects, we can claim that this meaning is specifically related to them. Therefore, we link the recognition to the specific images through the relation hasImage.

Finally, an individual of Iconographical Synthesis documents (crm:P70_documents) all the Iconological Recognitions conducted in the same descriptive situation.

Ontology description

Modeling principles

Through the term interpretation, we refer to an observer's interpretation of one or more artworks about their iconography and meaning. Other types of interpretations, such as those based on physical measurements of the artwork or its metadata definitions (e.g., dating, authorship, and title attribution) are not within the scope of this work.

An interpretation is modeled as a set of single recognitions of subjects occurring in a coherent situation. Recognitions made by different agents may be collected under the same interpretation. It is the case, for example, of art historians agreeing with other historians' claims, and basing their recognitions on the latter ones.

As described in the table below, in each level of interpretation the ontology makes a distinction between the description of the subject in the specific context of the artwork and the general subject as can be described by controlled vocabularies. Therefore, the object assigned by the recognition is not the general subject but the subjects as represented in the specific artwork considered (i.e. artistic motifs, images, intrinsic meanings). In this way, it is possible to describe peculiar details that the subjects show in a specific work of art and consequently describe their variations. For example, if an artwork shows Venus wearing a veil and earrings, which are not her common attributes, it is possible to affirm that only this representation of Venus in that specific artwork (i.e. the "image" of Venus at level 2) has these characteristics, and it does not imply that veil and earrings are the usual way in which the goddess is represented. The image of Venus can then be linked to an entity representing this subject at a general level, or to the corresponding vocabulary term, as the Iconclass code 92C4.

Recognition

This section describes how recognition is formally expressed, as shown in the figure.

We consider individuals of the class Recognition claims that are made by an agent (a person or a computer) about an artwork to recognize a single subject. The subclasses of Recognition express each recognition conducted at a certain level of interpretation, namely Pre-Iconographical Recognition and Formal Motif Recognition (level 1), Iconographical Recognition (level 2), and Iconological Recognition (level 3).

Each recognition of the single subjects made in a coherent situation is compliant with an Interpretation Description, a class representing the overall interpretation. This structure follows the Ontology Design Pattern of Situation and Description, to which the ontology is aligned by declaring, respectively, Recognition as a subclass of DOLCE-Ultralight class dul:Situation and InterpretationDescription as a subclass of dul:Description.

Each recognition is related to the artwork under examination through the relation aboutWorkOfArt. The class Artwork identifies the work of art as a conceptual, visual entity.

By aligning the class Recognition to other ontologies, it is possible to carefully express its sources and evidence (CiTO), type (HiCO), and agents (DOLCE, CIDOC-CRM).

Recognition assigns to the object of the recognition (the visual image of artwork or parts of it) the result of the recognition. For example, a second level recognition assigns to the level 1 subject "child with bow and arrows" (object of the recognition) the second level subject recognized, "Cupid" (the result of the recognition).

Level 1

Pre-iconographical Recognition

In a pre-iconographical recognition, the agent (interpreter) recognizes some artistic motifs in a work of art. These artistic motifs are associated physically with a portion of the painting and conceptually with the recognized first-level subjects, namely Natural Elements (e.g., person, sword), Actions (e.g., running, fighting), and Expressional Qualities (i.e., emotional aspects). The artistic motifs are the recognized subjects manifested in the artwork examined. Therefore, further qualities (e.g., color, number) can be described.

According to Panofsky's theory [1], the recognition of such subjects in the Preiocnographical level of interpretation is made through our experience of the world and does not require specific iconographic knowledge. For example, an observer recognizes a depicted figure as a woman (first level), before recognizing it as Venus (second level). Similarly, an observer who might not recognize Venus is still more likely to recognize a woman in a painting, given he/she had experience in knowing what women look like in his/her experience of the world. Artistic motifs that represent these recognized elements can be grouped inside compositions. A composition contains a series of artistic motifs. One can describe how the artistic motifs are grouped inside the composition through a compositional structure (e.g., pyramidal).

With our ontology, Pre-iconographical recognitions can be described as it follows. The n-ary relationship class PreiconographicalRecognition is linked either with the recognized artistic motif (ArtisticMotif) with the property recognizedArtisticMotif, or with a Composition of artistic motifs, through the relation recognizedComposition. To indicate the physical portion of the painting in which said composition or artistic motif is, one can relate a Composition or an ArtisticMotif to boundary boxes (which can be the result of an automatic recognition through computer vision algorithms) or an IIIF [2] specification of the image of the painting through the data property hasRegionDescription. A composition is linked to the artistic motifs that are part of it through the hasPart property. Its compositional structure (CompositionalStructure class) can be linked to it with the property hasCompositionalStructure. Finally, artistic motifs can be associated to the classes NaturalElement, Action, ExpressionalMeaning respectively with the properties hasNaturalMeaning (for natural elements and actions) and hasExpressionalMeaning. When further qualities of the artistic motif are identified, the class dul:Quality, describing them, can be linked to the artistic motif through the relation dul:hasQuality.

Each Preiconographical Recognition is related to the overall interpretation (i.e., InterpretationDescription) through the property preiconographicallyCompliesWith.

Formal Motif Recognition

In a formal motif recognition, the interpreter recognizes that a specific artistic motif (or composition) might have been copied from a prototypical artistic motif of another artwork. To express this concept in ICON, the prototypical motif used a reference is linked to the class Formal Motif Recognition, through the property hasPrototypicalMotif. The copied motif, that derives from the prototypical one, is linked to the FormalMotifRecognition class using the property hasCopiedMotif.

The Formal Motif Recognitions are related to the Interpretation Description of the copying artwork thrhough the relation preiconographicallyCompliesWith.

Both Pre-iconographical Recognitions and Formal Motif Recognitions classes and properties can be seen in the scheme representing level 1.

Level 2: Iconographical Recognition

Iconographical Recognition

In the second level of interpretation, an interpreter associates canonical Characters, Symbols, Events, Personifications, Named Objects to an artwork through the recognition of its Images. An Image is in fact the specific visual representation of one those aforementioned element in a painting. For example, although there is (arguably) one Virgin Mary concept as a character, its visual representation (image) changes from painting to painting. The recognition of these elements in an image is the heart of the Iconographic Recognition. Moreover, characters, named objects (i.e. uniquely identifiable objects that are often used as an attribute for other iconographic subjects, such as Excalibur, or Thor's hammer), symbols etc., take part into Invenzioni. An Invenzione is defined as a set of Characters, Events, Named Objects, Places, Symbols, Personifications having a narrative purpose (in this case an invenzione is called Story) or having an allegorical purpose (in this case it is called Allegory). Stories and Allegories can be recongnized in an artwork during the iconographic recongition. Furthermore, the Artistic Motif that refer to the recognized Image can be linked to them to not lose important connections between levels of interpretation. As to say, it is important to preserve the fact that the image that represent the character Zeus is associated to the artistic motif associated with a man in a painting, and with a bull in another painting (as olympic gods are sometimes depicted as wild animals).

To describe these concepts using ICON ontology we start with the N-ary relationship class IconographicalRecognition. The recognized images (Image class) are linked to a Recognition through the property recognizedImage. The recognized invenzioni (class Invenzione, with the sub-classes Story and Allegory), are linked to it with the property recognizedInvenzione. Every recognition is always dedicated to one element, so one image or invenzione is permitted per recognition. An image is linked to the corresponding artistic motif (or composition) in the pre-iconographic level through the property refersToArtisticMotif. If this artistic motif (or compositon) was the pivotal element to make a certain recognition of a second level subject, then that artistic motif is linked to the image through the property hasRecAttribute. Images are linked to the subjects that they visually represent with different properties according to the type of subject:

  • Place: hasPlace
  • Character: hasCharacter
  • Personification: hasPersonification
  • NamedObject: hasNamedObject
  • Symbol: hasSymbol. Symbols can be represented as Simulations [3] in this ontology. A simulation is considered as the relationship that happens between a symbol, its symbolic meaning and the context in which the symbol denotes the symbolic meaning. For example in a painting one might recognize that there is a dog as a symbol of fidelity. The simulation dog-fidelity would be linked to the painting. Simulations are expressed through the Simulation Ontology [4]. For more information about the use of the Simulation Ontology refer to its documentation.

Characters, places etc. are linked to the Invenzione in which they take part into using the property composedOf.

Finally, the class IconographicalAnalysis is linked to the iconographical recognitions made on an artwork with the cidoc-crm property crm:P70_Documents

Level 3: Iconological Recognition

The third level of interpretation concerns the understanding of deeper meanings of the artwork, such as its moral message, and the expression of a trait of the cultural context or the author (e.g., beliefs, attitudes, values)

In the third level of interpretation, an interpreter recognizes an intrinsic meaning in a work of art. This meaning can be linked to a specific element of the artwork (Artistic Motif, Image, Composition) or the whole artefact. Moreover, this intrinsic meaning might depend on a cultural phenomenon that is reflected in the artwork itself. The recognition of these elements are what makes an iconological recognition.

In ICON ontology, the class IconologicalRecognition expresses a third-level intepretation, that is linked to the recognized instrinsic meaning (class IntrinsicMeaning), namely the deeper meaning manifested into the artwork, through the property recognizedIntrinsicMeaning. The meaning itself can be either a general concept (e.g., "wedding wish") or a cultural phenomenon. In the first case ,it is expressed with the DOLCE class dul:SocialObject and it is linked to the intrinsic meaning with the property recognizedConcept. In the second case, the CulturalPhenomenon associated to the intrinsic meaning is linked to it with the property recognizedCulturalPhenomenon. At the moment, the class CulturalPhenomenon has as subclasses Tendency, Belief, Attitude, CulturalValue. No further specification for these classes is provided as they might be thoroughly described in specific ontologies to be developed. If the intrinsic meaning is recognized particularly thanks to a specific artistic motif, composition or image, or particularly such parts of the artwork, it is linked to them respectively thorugh the properties hasArtisticMotif, hasComposition and hasImage. Finally, the relation iconologicallyCompliesWith relates all the iconological recognitions to the Interpretation Description representing the complete interpretation collecting every recognition at each level.

ICON 2.0 extension description

As anticipated in the introduction, version 2.0 introduces new shortcuts, expressed as property chains, to relate the artwork to the recognized subjects at the first, second, or third level of interpretation. For each level, a property is created, respectively preiconographicallyDepicts for level 1, iconographicallyDepicts for level 2, and iconologicallyRepresents for level 3.

The property chains are represented by the following Manchester syntax:

  • preiconographicallyDepicts:
    • hasRecognition o recognizedArtisticMotif o hasFactualMeaning
    • hasRecognition o recognizedArtisticMotif o hasExpressionalMeaning
    • hasRecognition o recognizedArtisticMotif o crm:P138_represents
    • hasRecognition o recognizedComposition o hasPart o hasFactualMeaning
    • hasRecognition o recognizedComposition o hasPart o hasExpressionalMeaning
    • hasRecognition o recognizedComposition o hasPart o crm:P138_represents
  • iconographicallyDepicts:
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasCharacter
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasPersonification
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasEvent
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasPlace
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasSymbol
    • hasRecognition o recognizedImage o hasNamedObject
    • hasRecognition o recognizedInvenzione
    • hasRecognition o recognizedInvenzione o composedOf
  • iconologicallyRepresents:
    • hasRecognition o recognizedIntrinsicMeaning o recognizedCulturalPhenomenon
    • hasRecognition o recognizedIntrinsicMeaning o recognizedConcept

As shown in the diagram below, preiconographicallyDepicts is defined as a superproperty of the chain of properties linking an artwork to the identified level 1 subjects. Several paths are included, namely: hasRecognition (the inverse property of aboutWorkOfArt), recognizedArtisticMotif or recognizedComposition, and hasExpressionalMeaning or icon:hasFactualMeaning or crm:P138_represents. The property crm:P138_represents is included in the chain, as it is the most appropriate property to describe what a visual object depicts that one can use to integrate the ontology when none of the available properties are suitable for describing the subject identified (e.g., writings on the pictorial surface).

Similarly, preiconographicallyDepicts includes the chain of properties linking the artwork to the second-level subjects. Several chains are identified, including 1) the identification of the image and the possible subjects associated with it (hasRecognition, recognizedImage, hasCharacter or hasPersonification, etc.), 2) the identification of the invenzione composed of the second level subjects (hasRecognition,recognizedInvenzione, composedOf) or a simple recognition of an invenzione (hasRecognition, recognizedInvenzione).

Finally, the property iconologicallyRepresents is defined as a superproperty of the property chain relating the artwork to the third-level subjects, namely hasRecognition, recognizedIntrinsicMeaning, recognizedCulturalPhenomenon or recognizedConcept.

Being defined as property chains, it is possible to infer them from data described with the ontology 1.1 version.

Example description

As a way of example of ontology usage, the description of two reliefs on the external wall of Saint Mark's Basilica in Venice will be described, and their formal description will be visually represented at each level of interpretation.

This example is considered by Panofsky and Saxl[5, p. 228]. The two scholars noticed that two reliefs (figures 1 and 2) belonging to different historical periods and representing different subjects clearly present the same visual arrangement. The first relief is recognized by them as a late roman one, representing the story of Hercules Carrying the Herymanthian boar, whereas the second one, from the XIII Century A.D., represents an allegory of Christ saving the christian souls.

In the authors' interpretation, it is evident that the medieval artist used the roman relief as a model for representing a new subject, closer to their culture. Since, during the Middle Ages, there is other evidence in which a classical artwork is used as a model for contemporary subjects, deprived of its classical content, they claim that these artworks are the evidence of a cultural phenomenon happening. They conclude that Medieval Western art was unable to retain a classical prototype without destroying its original meaning since they were not capable of realizing the unity of classical form and classical subject matter.

The table below summarizes how the artwork can be described at each level of interpretation.

In the first artwork (figure 1), a nude man with a lion skin carries on his shoulders a boar. In the lower right corner, another figure takes shelter in a barrel. At the second level of interpretation these level 1 subjects can be grouped and recognized: the man is Hercules with his attribute "lion skin" and the man in the barrel is king Eurystheus.

The same interpretative process can be applied to the other artwork (figure 2), where the man is recognized as Christ, the deer as the symbol of the Christians' souls, and the dragoon in the corner is recognized as symbol of the Devil. His actions of carrying the deer and of stepping on the Devil are respectively symbolically interpreted as the act saving the souls and defeating the Devil. In the last level, the cultural phenomenon described above is recognized as belonging to the medieval artifact.

Example: level 1

Example: Pre-iconographical recognition

To better explain the ontology usage, we describe how the preiconographical recognition can be applied to the first level of interpretation of the example described in the section example description.

The relief representing "Hercules carrying the Erymanthian Boar" shows, at the first level of interpretation, a man wearing a lion skin carrying on his shoulders a boar. We know that a man with a lion skin will be interpreted as the character Hercules at the second level of interpretation. Then, the man taking shelter in a barrel will be interpreted as King Euryshteus the the second level. The first man is also doing the action he makes of carrying a boar on his shoulders. Finally, the overall scene will be interpreted as the story of Hercules carrying the Erymanthian boar to king Eurystheus.

These level 1 objects can be grouped in different compositions. A composition is hence a unity of artistic motifs that have a sense together, needed for creating a correspondence between the second and the first level subjects. Compositions can be also used for indicating the objects involved in an action as well, and it is not required that every composition is futher interpreted in the second level of interpretation. As a result, we obtain four compositions:

  • composition 1: man and lion skin
  • composition 2: man, action of carrying, boar
  • composition 3: man, taking shelter, barrel
  • composition 4: all the Artistic Motifs identified

The recognition is modelled as follows. The authors (dul:includesAgent) recognize (PreiconographicalRecognition) a Composition, which is composed (hasPart) by ArtisticMotifs, i.e. the specific manifestation in the artwork of the subjects represented. The Artistic Motifs are then related to the general subjects and qualities that are recognized. For example, to the Artistic Motif related to the subject "man" can be linked the quality (dul:Quality) "nudity". In this way, it is possible to affirm that only the man as represented in this artwork has "nudity" as quality, and not all the representations of "man" in other artworks are likely to have the same quality.

The text from which the intepretation is extracted can be indicated as source of the interpretation (cito:citesForInformation).

The recognitions are about (icon:aboutWorkOfArt) the artwork under examination (ART1), and are declared as part of the overall interpretation ART1-DESC1, having type icon:InterpretationDescription through the relation icon:preiconographicallyCompliesWith. To foster the retrieval of subjects, the property chain icon:preiconographicallyDepicts, acting as a shortcut, relates the artwork to the subjects recognized at the first level of recognition.

The same approach can be used to describe the second relief of the example.

Example: Formal Motif Recognition

We describe how the Formal Motif Recognition can be applied to the first level of interpretation of the example described in the section example description.

Since Saxl and Panofsky claim that the artist making the medieval relief on the Saint Mark's Basilica wall copied the shape and general arrangement from the roman one, a Formal Motif Recognition occurs. Therefore, the composition identifying all the scene represented can be related to the composition representing the classical scene. In addition, more recognitions can identify the exact correspondence between:

  • the deer and the boar, respectively identified by the artistic motifs ART2-AM3 and ART1-AM3
  • Christ and Hercules, respectively identified by the compositions ART2-COMP1 and ART1-COMP1
  • the dragoon and king Eurystheus, respectively identified by the artistic motif ART2-AM6 and ART1-COMP3

As shown in the scheme, each FormalMotifRecognition is about (aboutWorkOfArt) both the artworks involved. In this case, Saxl and Panofsky are always the authors of the recognition. The parts of the artwork taken as a prototype (e.g. the Roman relief, here indicated as ART1), namely Artistic Motifs and Composiitons, are related to the interpetation with the property hasPrototypicalMotif, whereas the corresponding parts of the final copying artwork (e.g. the Medieval relief, , here indicated as ART2) are related through the property hasCopiedMotif. In this way, the direct correspondence between level 1 levels can be expressed. All recognitions are listed in the InterpretationDescription of the copying artwork (ART1004).

In this case, composition 1 is recognized as the character Hercules, which is a character corresponding to Image 1. The Artistic Motif identifying the lion skin is related to the image by the relation hasRecAttribute, since it is the attribute allowing the character recognition. Then, Artistic Motif 3, identifying a not further defined "boar", is linked to Image 2 and interpreted as the Erymanthian Boar, i.e. a level 2 Character. Therefore, the general Characters identified are recognized as part of the general story "Hercules carrying the Erymanthian Boar to king Eurystheus". The story is then linked to the artwork and to the level 1 composition identifying the scene represented by means of another IconographicalRecognition recognizing the Invenzione (recognizedInvenzione).

Example: Iconological Recognition

In the example described, it is recognized by Saxl and Panofsky that both the artworks are involved in a common cultural phenomenon. In detail, the phenomenon regards specifically the Medieval relief, since it concerns the way in which Medieval artists deal with the Classic art. Therefore, we can say that the phenomenon is embedded in the Medieval relief, and the roman one is evidence of it.

In addition, this recognition is supported by the fact that there is a copy of formal motifs done by the Medieval artist, who took the Roman relief as a prototype for his artwork. Therefore, it is possible to affirm that the Formal Motif Recognitions made at level 2 support the level 3 interpretation, identifying the cultural phenomenon.

At the third level of interpretation, it can be also recognized that the Allegory of Salvation expresses the concept of Redemption, as part of the meanings belonging to the Christian beliefs. Consequently, another recognition linking this deeper meaning to the artwork should be considered.

This interpretative situation can be modelled as follows (see the scheme for its representation). The authors make a third-level recognition, i.e. an IconologicalRecognition, and recognize that the Artwork representing an Allegory of Salvation has an Intrinsic Meaning (IconologicalRecognition, recognizedIntrinsicMeaning, IntrinsicMeaning). This Intrinsic Meaning is then linked to the CulturalPhenomenon labeled as "the medieval mind was incapable of realizing the unity of classical form and classical subject matter". The Intrinsic Meaning is indeed the specific manifestation of the general phenomenon recognized. The Motifs Recognitions already conducted at level 1 that are described in the scheme of a Formal Motif Recognition (level 1), support this interpretation by means of the relation cito:givesSupportTo. The IconologicalRecognition, then, cites as evidence (cito:citesAsEvidence) the Roman relief.

Following, a further iconological recognition links to the artwork another instance of Intrinsic Meaning, which is related to the concept of "redemption". Since this interpretation can be seen in the second-level subjects, we can claim that this meaning is specifically related to them. Therefore, we link the recognition to the specific images through the relation hasImage.

Finally, all the Iconological Recognitions conducted in the same descriptive situation are related to the overall Interpretation Description through the property iconologicallyCompliesWith. The shortcut iconologicallyRepresents directly relates the recognized third-level subjects to the artwork.

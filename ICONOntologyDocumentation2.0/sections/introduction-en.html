Introduction back to ToC

The ICON ontology defines classes and properties to express artistic interpretations of figurative artworks' subject matter (i.e., iconographies) and meanings (i.e., symbols, iconological aspects). It was developed by conceptualizing authoritative knowledge and notions from art historians' theoretical approaches. The ontology creation is motivated by the need to provide a comprehensive description of the content of the artwork, thereby attributing subjectivity, responsibility, and sources to each statement while representing both iconographical and iconological aspects. This allows for the representation and integration of complex domain-specific knowledge into the context of the semantic web.

ICON is a domain-specific ontology intended to be used by 1) museums aiming at describing thoroughly the content of the artworks, 2) art historians conducting research with the aim of quantitative approaches, and 3) developers using computer vision to associate a content description to portions of artworks.

The ontology structure is based on the theory described by Panofsky as expressed in Studies in Iconology [1], who subdivided the act of interpretation into three layers, i.e., pre-iconographical, iconographical and iconological. The subject is interpreted in each layer with an increasing level of complexity. For example, a child with wings, bow, and arrows (level 1) is recognized at level 2 as the character Cupid, and at the third level, a cultural meaning can be associated with its specific representation in a certain artwork, e.g. a good omen for a wedding in Lorenzo Lotto's Venus and Cupid. By adopting this theory, this ontology aims at formally representing the subjects represented in artworks, their variations, and meanings, with a high level of granularity. The objects that it is possible to describe at each level are 1) natural elements (e.g. people, objects), expressional qualities (i.e., emotions), actions, qualities (e.g. color, pose), spatial arrangement (e.g. three people arranged in a pyramidal structure); 2) characters, specific places and objects (e.g. the city of Paris or the Bible book), personifications, symbols, allegories, stories; 3) meanings and cultural phenomena; 4) and intra-levels relations. At the current state, ICON allows expressing the subjectiveness and responsibility of the attributions for each element recognized in the artwork, along with the sources from which the claim is extracted and the evidence on which it is based. In this way, multiple statements about the same artwork can be modeled.

Its main classes have been aligned to ontologies that come from the domains of cultural descriptions (ArCo, CIDOC-CRM, VIR), semiotics (DOLCE), bibliometrics (CITO), and symbolism (Simulation Ontology), to grant a robust schema that can be extendable using additional classes and properties coming from these ontologies.

A thorough description of the ontology and its development can be found in the following article:

Sartini, B., Baroncini, S., van Erp, M., Tomasi, F., & Gangemi, A. (2023). ICON: An Ontology for Comprehensive Artistic Interpretations. Journal on Computing and Cultural Heritage. https://doi.org/10.1145/3594724

Version 2.0

The ontology version 2.0 introduces new super-properties of chains that allow for easier navigation between artworks and their respective interpretation subjects. The property chains relate the artwork directly to its first, second, or third-level identified subjects. Therefore, the shortcut can be used 1) when further information about the provenance of the interpretation is missing and no further detail about the depicted subject is provided, and 2) as a shortcut to improve information retrieval of data describing each recognition. By using these properties, the extended ontology aims to simplify the process of connecting artworks with their associated subjects and cultural contexts, making it more efficient and streamlined.

No further changes to the classes and properties defined in the previous version were applied. Therefore, the current version is fully compatible with the previous release (version 1.1)

A description of the ontology extension and usage examples is illustrated in:

Baroncini, S., & Sartini, B. (2023, November 7). A Comparative Study of Simple and Complex Art Interpretations in Linked Open Data Using ICON Ontology. SWODCH’23: International Workshop on Semantic Web and Ontology Design for Cultural Heritage, Athens. https://ceur-ws.org/Vol-3540/paper4.pdf
